# Practical Writing and Revision

This skill provides guidance for planning, drafting, rewriting, and revising English prose using practical principles distilled from *The Penguin Writer’s Manual*. Focus on functional and academic writing first—emails, letters, memos, reports, presentations, essays, summaries, and similar prose—while applying many of the same principles to general nonfiction writing.

The central idea is simple: writing is deliberate communication across distance. The writer has time to think, shape tone, revise, and correct. Use that advantage. Good writing usually comes less from ornament than from preparation, selection, and pruning.

## Core Model

Treat every writing task as a combination of six questions:

1. What is the exact purpose?
2. Who is the reader?
3. What does the reader already know?
4. What action, belief, or understanding should remain after reading?
5. What level of formality suits the situation?
6. What constraints apply: length, medium, deadline, evidence, layout, or institutional conventions?

Before drafting, reduce the task to a statement of intent in one sentence, or two at most. Keep it concrete. Examples:

- Request approval for a budget increase.
- Explain a technical issue to a non-technical client.
- Argue that one interpretation of a poem is stronger than another.
- Summarize the main findings of a report and recommend next steps.

Use the statement of intent as a check on every later choice.

## First Principles of Good Style

Judge every draft against six qualities:

### Clarity

Put clarity first. Make the reader understand the message without undue effort.

To achieve clarity:

- Know exactly what the text is trying to say before polishing the wording.
- Use grammar and punctuation to remove ambiguity.
- Make the main point easy to find.
- Match explanation and terminology to the reader’s knowledge.
- Distinguish clearly between fact, analysis, conclusion, and recommendation.

Treat clarity as the governing principle for all later revisions.

### Simplicity

Prefer simple expression where it says the same thing accurately.

Apply simplicity by:

- Choosing the shorter, more ordinary, more concrete word when no meaning is lost.
- Preferring verbs over inflated noun phrases.
- Replacing bureaucratic phrasing with direct language.
- Using technical terms only when they are genuinely the right terms.
- Explaining unfamiliar terms, acronyms, or specialist language on first use.

Do not force everything into childish monosyllables. Keep necessary precision. The rule is not “always short”; it is “as simple as accuracy allows.”

### Economy

Save the reader’s time.

Apply economy by:

- Cutting repetition, throat-clearing, padding, and needless scene-setting.
- Removing redundant modifiers and doubled expressions.
- Saying the necessary thing once, clearly.
- Repeating a key word when repetition improves clarity instead of hunting for weaker synonyms.
- Starting the real message early, especially in practical or business writing.

Economy is not abruptness. Keep enough wording to remain clear and courteous.

### Variety

Hold attention through variation.

Create variety by:

- Mixing shorter and longer sentences.
- Varying paragraph length where the subject allows.
- Avoiding identical sentence openings and mechanical rhythms.
- Adjusting tone and pace to the material.

Avoid monotony at both extremes: endless short statements feel childish or breathless, while uninterrupted long sentences become muddy and exhausting.

### Vigour

Keep prose alive.

To give writing vigour:

- Stay close to concrete actions, specific nouns, and energetic verbs.
- Allow some natural voice to remain, even in formal prose.
- Use emphasis deliberately rather than constantly.
- Choose vivid wording only when the matter itself justifies it.

Do not inflate the ordinary into the sensational. Strong language should be earned by the subject, not pasted on for effect.

### Suitability

Make style fit purpose, reader, and form.

Suitability governs choices about tone, degree of formality, personal versus impersonal phrasing, use of jargon, and acceptable level of emotional charge. What suits an internal memo may fail in an academic essay; what suits a friendly email may sound careless in a complaint to a supplier.

## Working Method

Follow this sequence unless the task clearly calls for a different one.

### 1. Identify purpose and reader

Infer the likely reader if the user does not specify one. Ask internally:

- Is the reader busy?
- Is the reader expert, mixed, or non-specialist?
- Is the reader friendly, neutral, skeptical, or resistant?
- Must the reader decide, approve, understand, apologize, buy, or act?

Assume the reader is not a machine. Preserve a human touch.

### 2. Gather and sort material

Separate:

- core point
- necessary facts
- supporting evidence
- objections or complications
- required action
- material that can be cut or moved to notes, appendices, or later paragraphs

For summaries, identify the original author’s main points and stance before compressing.

### 3. Plan before drafting

Make a brief outline, even for short prose. A list of points is enough.

General planning rules:

- In functional writing, state the main intention early.
- In persuasive writing, present major supporting points in a clear order.
- In chronological or process writing, use sequence.
- In compare/contrast writing, group similarities and differences deliberately.
- In analytical writing, arrange points by logic, not merely by discovery order.
- In essays, organize the body first, then write the introduction and conclusion.

When possible, build the plan on a paragraph-by-paragraph basis.

### 4. Draft in a natural voice

Draft continuously enough to preserve momentum. Let the first draft be workable rather than perfect.

During the first draft:

- Preserve the user’s meaning and evidence.
- Avoid inventing facts.
- Prefer the writer’s natural voice as a starting point.
- Avoid heavy line-by-line editing while drafting.

### 5. Revise by pruning and reshaping

Assume that style often improves by taking away. Revise at three levels:

- words
- sentences
- paragraphs

Then perform technical checks for spelling, grammar, usage, punctuation, abbreviations, formatting, and consistency.

## Word-Level Guidance

### Choose the right word

Choose the word or phrase that expresses the meaning most clearly, simply, and forcefully for the context.

Prefer the simpler term when both are accurate. Keep the longer or more technical term only when it adds needed precision, carries established disciplinary meaning, or suits the intended audience.

### Avoid needless complexity

Watch for:

- inflated diction
- abstract noun clusters
- fake formality
- bureaucratic phrasing
- euphemisms that blur reality

Replace “endeavour to ensure” with “make sure” when no nuance is lost. Replace “additional premium” style language only where precision does not demand it.

### Control jargon

Treat jargon as private language. Use it freely only when writing for insiders who genuinely share it.

When writing for broader audiences:

- define unfamiliar specialist terms
- spell out acronyms at first mention
- replace insider shorthand with plain language where possible

If unsure whether a term is jargon, imagine an intelligent outsider reading it.

### Avoid clichés and buzzwords

Avoid stale expressions, empty business catchphrases, and fashionable terms used mainly to sound current.

Clichés substitute ready-made phrasing for actual thought. Buzzwords date quickly and may confuse readers outside the relevant circle. Prefer direct wording over formulaic rhetoric.

### Distinguish informal language from slang

Use informal language only when the relationship and medium permit it. Keep slang out of most serious writing.

For academic, business, and formal public writing:

- avoid casual shortenings
- avoid contractions unless the genre clearly allows them
- avoid trendy, racy, or fast-dating vocabulary

For friendly messages, moderate informality is fine if it sounds natural.

### Control emotive force

Choose between neutral and emotionally charged language deliberately.

Use neutral wording for balanced analysis, reporting, and explanation. Use more emotive wording only when the task genuinely calls for warning, persuasion, encouragement, criticism, or urgency.

Never smuggle judgment into supposedly neutral prose by accident.

## Tone

Tone is the written equivalent of tone of voice. It cannot be avoided, only managed.

Choose tone deliberately by deciding:

- formal or informal
- neutral or persuasive
- friendly or distant
- personal or impersonal
- calm or urgent

Then keep the tone consistent. Do not lurch between registers.

For most professional writing, aim for language that is formal enough to command confidence but not so stiff that it becomes pompous, evasive, or impersonal.

### Personal and impersonal style

Do not assume impersonality is automatically more serious.

In many contexts, clear personal phrasing is stronger than evasive abstract phrasing. Prefer an honest “I recommend” or “We propose” to shapeless constructions like “it is felt that” when agency matters.

In academic writing, greater tentativeness and some impersonality may suit the task, but vary the phrasing and avoid overloading the prose with passive constructions and empty expletives such as “it is argued that” unless attribution or caution genuinely requires them.

### Active and passive voice

Prefer active verbs for directness and vigour.

Use passive voice when:

- the doer is unknown
- the doer is irrelevant
- the receiver of the action deserves emphasis
- institutional convention makes passive phrasing more natural

Choose voice deliberately. Do not let passive constructions erase useful information.

## Sentence-Level Guidance

There is no fixed ideal sentence length. Prefer shorter sentences by default, but use longer ones when the logic is clear and the structure holds.

Revise sentences by:

- shortening tangled sentences
- combining overly choppy sequences
- keeping subject and verb relationships clear
- repeating a key word when it prevents confusion
- using punctuation to aid sense and rhythm

Read mentally for rhythm. Smooth out stumbles, clutter, and awkward repetition. Use short sentences for emphasis, pace, and impact. Use longer sentences for qualified thought, provided they remain controlled.

## Paragraph-Level Guidance

Treat the paragraph as the unit of thought.

General rule: one idea, one paragraph.

Apply paragraphing by:

- giving each paragraph a clear subject
- opening with a topic sentence when that helps navigation
- keeping related material together
- breaking large blocks into usable units
- ensuring progression from one paragraph to the next

Use paragraphs to help both planning and reading. A pressed reader should be able to track the argument by following paragraph openings.

For dialogue, put each speaker in a separate paragraph.

## Task-Specific Patterns

### Emails and business letters

For letters and emails:

- begin with a clear subject line or heading
- state why the message is being sent near the beginning
- develop one point per paragraph
- end with a clear action point, request, or next step
- keep the tone courteous, plain, and controlled
- avoid ponderous commercialese
- avoid false familiarity

For email specifically:

- include a greeting and sign-off unless the context clearly allows omission
- never “shout” in all caps
- mention attachments clearly
- copy only the people who need the message

### Memos

Make memos brief and unmistakable.

Put the action required near the start. Use numbering when it speeds comprehension. Strip out personal irritation, rambling narrative, and ceremonial phrasing.

### Reports

Separate the parts of a report clearly:

- scope or terms of reference
- background
- facts or findings
- analysis
- conclusions
- recommendations
- appendices or references where needed

Write the summary last. Use headings and subheadings to make the structure visible. Distinguish objective findings from opinion and recommendation.

### Presentations

Write for listeners as well as readers.

For presentations:

- simplify language further than in silent reading prose
- build in signposting, repetition, and recap
- avoid talking down to the audience
- match tone to audience size and familiarity
- make visual aids support the message rather than replace it

### Essays and academic writing

For essays:

- analyse the title carefully, especially command words such as describe, analyse, compare, discuss, and explain
- organize the body before writing the introduction
- use the introduction to define scope and approach
- make the conclusion follow from the argument rather than merely repeat it
- avoid informal wording and unsupported generalizations
- keep paragraph links explicit
- attribute quotations and references accurately

Academic style should still be good style: clear, simple, economical, and well organized. Let caution and neutrality appear through careful wording, not through shapeless prose.

### Summaries

When summarizing:

- identify the main points before condensing
- preserve the source’s stance and proportions
- distinguish major points from supporting detail
- compress without distorting
- avoid importing approval, skepticism, or emotional colour absent from the original

Summarizing well is also an editing skill. Use it to discover what can be cut from longer prose without breaking the structure.

## Revision Checklist

Before presenting a final draft, check the following:

- Is the exact purpose clear?
- Is the intended reader clear?
- Does the opening reach the main point early enough?
- Does each paragraph contain one main idea?
- Are facts, analysis, and recommendations clearly separated where necessary?
- Can any word be simpler without loss of meaning?
- Is any jargon unexplained?
- Is any cliché, buzzword, slang, or fake formality weakening the prose?
- Is the tone consistent?
- Are active and passive constructions chosen deliberately?
- Are sentence lengths varied enough to avoid monotony?
- Are spelling, grammar, punctuation, abbreviations, names, dates, and numbers correct?
- Does the ending give the reader a clear conclusion, impression, or action point?

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Writing before deciding the purpose

Bad writing often starts as undirected material. Define the exact purpose first, then shape the prose around it.

### Mistake 2: Hiding the main point

Do not bury the request, conclusion, or recommendation deep in the text unless the form specifically requires delayed revelation.

### Mistake 3: Mistaking formality for quality

Long words, abstract nouns, and passive constructions do not automatically make prose sound intelligent. They often make it sound evasive.

### Mistake 4: Mixing tones

Do not swing between stiff official language and casual chat unless a deliberate rhetorical effect is required.

### Mistake 5: Confusing vividness with overstatement

Give writing life, but do not exaggerate the ordinary into drama.

### Mistake 6: Letting paragraphs become blocks

Large unbroken walls of text tire the reader and hide structure. Break them by idea.

## Output Guidance for AI Use

When applying this skill to a user request:

1. Preserve the user’s meaning unless the user asks for substantive change.
2. Improve language before changing argument.
3. Make assumptions only when necessary, and keep them conservative.
4. Prefer complete rewrites when the user asks for polished copy.
5. Prefer targeted comments when the user asks for feedback, critique, or teaching.
6. For summaries, compress faithfully.
7. For formal documents, produce copy that is usable with minimal further editing.

The final aim is not to sound grand. The final aim is to communicate clearly, simply, economically, vividly where needed, and in a tone that suits the task.
